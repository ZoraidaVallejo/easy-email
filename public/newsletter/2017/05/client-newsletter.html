How To Hide Content from Search Engines, and Why You Want To

In general, web-based marketing is all about getting search engines to index your content. However, there are certain circumstances when you may not want search engines to index some of your content. Learn about the robots exclusion standard, when and how to use it, and some of the nuances associated with using it and other exclusion protocols as part of your overall marketing strategy.


Tech News

As Trump’s FCC Boss Looks to Kill Today’s Net Neutrality Laws, Silicon Valley Companies are Starting to Push Back

A group of technology companies, including Google, Facebook, and Netflix, recently met with FCC chair Ajit Pai to express support for current net neutrality rules, which Pai is expected to eliminate in the coming months. Under current rules, an internet service provider cannot give preferential treatment to certain content providers over others, nor can it require content providers to pay for faster delivery to consumers.

Why It Matters That Google Home Can Now Identify You by Voice

Google recently updated its voice-activated home hub to distinguish between different speakers. While some voice assistants, like Apple’s Siri, are typically used on personal devices by one individual, home hubs are often accessible to multiple people, making it more important to distinguish between speakers. The feature is primarily aimed at reducing the incidence of accidental (or unauthorized) commands.

“Unenforceable”: How Voluntary Net Neutrality Lets ISPs Call the Shots

In light of expectations that FCC chair Ajit Pai will eliminate the FCC’s classification of internet service providers (ISPs) as common carriers under Title II of the Communications Act, this article explains the limitations of “voluntary” net neutrality, which is the proposed alternative. Specifically, if net neutrality is shifted from the FCC to the FTC, then ISPs would only be bound by the specific promises they choose to make, and it is unclear how those might be enforced or maintained.

162 Tech Companies File Brief Against the Latest Immigration Executive Order

In mid-April, 162 technology companies—including Google, Facebook, Amazon, Microsoft, and Yahoo—filed a joint amicus brief in support of a lawsuit challenging President Trump’s immigration order issued in March. The brief claims that the executive order violates federal anti-discrimination law and is overbroad in application, banning millions of people who “could not plausibly be foreign terrorists.”

Torching the Modern-Day Library of Alexandria

This article chronicles the concept, growth, litigation, and demise of Google’s ambitious “Project Ocean,” which sought to build a digital library by scanning every book in the world. The struggle reflects the complexity and nuance of copyright in a digital age, and the sometimes-competing, sometimes-complementary interests of individual authors, large publishing companies, and a giant technology company that once urged to itself “don’t be evil.”

SEO News

Harnessing the Power of Online Reviews

Local businesses increasingly depend on online reviews to attract customers. In fact, studies show that more than half of prospective customers trust user-generated content (such as customer reviews) over information found on a company website or in news articles. To that end, this article offers advice on handling bad reviews (view them as a business issue, not a review issue) and cultivating good reviews.

Fact Check Now Available in Google Search and News Around the World

Google has continued its efforts to help users differentiate between factually accurate and false statements from online sources. The search giant has expanded the Fact Check feature it rolled out on a limited basis last fall to all of Google Search and News worldwide. However, this feature won’t be available in all searches, and in order for publishers to be included they must use a specific fact-checking schema markup or tool.

Google to Include Built-in Ad Blocker in Chrome Browser

Recent reports indicate that Google plans to include an ad-blocker in its Chrome browser, despite the fact that Google itself sells digital ads. The apparent reason for this move is a change in ad standards put forth by an industry group that has identified ads such as pop-ups and auto-playing videos as being “beneath a threshold of consumer acceptability.” In light of this development as well as the proliferation of third-party ad blockers, reports speculate that Google’s strategy is to filter out bad ads in order to make users more receptive to higher quality ads and therefore less likely to block them.

Google Brings ‘Your Timeline’ to iOS: A Searchable History of Your Life

Google is now providing users with a searchable history of locations they have physically visited via its ‘Your Timeline’ feature in the iOS version of Google Maps. This tool, which in the past was only available on the Android system, allows users to not only retrace their steps, but also to edit or delete information from their location timelines. It also stores information about all of a user’s past visits to a particular location for review via one succinct ‘Place Card.’

Social Media News

YouTube’s New $35-a-Month Live TV Service Feels Like a Missed Opportunity for Google

Seen as a significant step forward by many consumers who would like to live in a post-cable world, some industry observers feel that YouTube’s new subscription TV service doesn’t quite live up to its potential. Specifically, the separation that exists between YouTube TV and classic YouTube content is said to limit user experience. This separation is reported to eliminate many of the benefits historically associated with classic YouTube, and which YouTube TV could utilize to set itself apart from competitors.

Deaths on ‘Facebook Live’ Are Becoming a Worrying Trend

Since Facebook Live was introduced last year, a number of people have intentionally and unintentionally broadcast their own or other people’s deaths via the social media company’s live streaming feature. These videos and any associated news stories have apparently become the most popular Facebook Live content, signaling an alarming trend in how the service is being used.

Snapchat Will Tell Brands How Many People Saw Their Ads, Then Visited Their Stores, Restaurants

Following in Facebook’s footsteps, Snapchat has unveiled a new feature that will allow businesses to track how many people who have seen their ads subsequently patronize their brick-and-mortar locations. The app will only be able to identify those users if they open Snapchat while they are physically at the business location whose ads they have viewed. In addition to this tracking feature, Snapchat plans to roll out tools aimed at pushing users to visit businesses based on various identifiers.

YouTube Channels With Under 10K Views Can No Longer Display Ads

In an effort to drive traffic away from lower quality content, YouTube has restricted advertisements to only those channels with at least 10,000 views. This move was apparently prompted when several top brands removed their ads from channels displaying questionable videos.

FTC Tells ‘Influencers’ to Quit Trying to Hide the Fact That They’re Shilling for Brands

Some of Instagram’s so-called ‘influencers’ are running afoul of the Federal Trade Commission. Recently, the FTC sent out nearly 100 letters, putting celebrities, athletes, and other influencers on notice for failing to properly document that some of their posts are effectively sponsorships for which they are receiving compensation. The agency also outlined specific expectations about where the sponsor notice should appear in Instagram posts and provided examples of inadequate documentation (e.g., putting one’s sponsor message under the “more” button where few users would see it or using obscure terminology in hashtags).

Facebook Is Building Brain-Computer Interfaces for Typing and Skin-Hearing

At Facebook’s annual F8 event, the company revealed that a team of engineers is working to build a brain-computer interface that would make it possible to type with just your mind—no invasive implants required. The goal is to use optical imaging to scan your brain and detect when you’re speaking silently in your head, then translate that into text. Facebook hopes that this “direct brain interface” will also one day allow people to control augmented and virtual reality experiences without the use of a screen or controller.

Secure Client Communication in the Trump Era

Recently, Congress passed, and President Trump signed into law, a bill that repeals the Federal Communications Commission rules. As a result, your internet service provider (ISP) is now allowed to legally track your unprotected web browsing history and sell it to interested parties. If you are an attorney, it is understandable you may now have some concerns about attorney-client confidentiality. There are steps you can take to further ensure acceptable confidentiality when corresponding with clients, however, including making use of secure client portals, using a VPN, and choosing an ISP that respects your privacy.

Law Firms Must Manage Cybersecurity Risks

Advancements in technology are changing the way lawyers and law firms handle electronic security. Firewalls can be employed that block employee access to specific websites due to malware risks. Cloud-based services allow users to send files online securely, and software is available to quarantine emails with suspicious wording or attachments. These suggestions for security advancement are not without justification, as two of the largest law firms in the United States were caught in a major cybersecurity breach in 2016, and malware is ever-evolving.

Overcoming Objections to the Cloud in the Legal Sector

Cloud computing and storage are fast-becoming web-based standards in the professional realm, yet many organizations view security as a barrier to moving to the cloud. This rings especially true for law firms that are concerned with how to handle sensitive information without compromising their clients’ confidentiality. Despite popular misconceptions, however, the cloud offers many more stringent security features than a law firm could hope to implement for its own IT infrastructure and manage on-site.
