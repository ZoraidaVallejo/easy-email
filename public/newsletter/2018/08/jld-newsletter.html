Tech News

Europe Hits Google With Record $5 Billion Antitrust Fine Over Bundling of Its Apps on Android

A regulatory body of the European Union fined Google $5 billion for bundling its own search and web browsing apps on devices running its Android operating system. The regulators gave Google 90 days to restructure its relationships with device makers and submit fixes for their approval. Although the ruling pertains to Android users in the European Union, Google may determine that it is too cumbersome to have different policies for different markets and thus may make changes to devices worldwide.

The 21st Century Internet Act Aims to Enshrine Net Neutrality in Law

Reacting to the FCC’s repeal of “net neutrality” rules last December, Representative Mike Coffman (R-CO) has proposed a bill that would provide for internet openness, albeit via legislation rather than by regulation. The “21st Century Internet Act,” would modify the Communications Act of 1934 and the 1996 Telecommunications Act to add a host of new provisions specifically applicable to internet service providers (ISPs). As the now-repealed net neutrality rules did, the Act would ban ISPs from blocking, throttling, or prioritizing content on the basis of how much the content provider pays the ISP.

ACLU Calls for a Moratorium on Government Use of Facial Recognition Technologies

The ACLU has called for a moratorium on the use of facial recognition technology by government agencies. Companies like Amazon, Google, and Microsoft have faced criticism for selling facial recognition technology to the government before the implications of such technology can be adequately studied and discussed.

FTC Says It Will Take a ‘Close Look’ At EU Antitrust Fine Against Google

FTC Chairman Joseph Simons stated that the commission will be reviewing the EU decision to levy a $5 billion fine against Google for alleged antitrust violations due to its bundling of search and browser apps with its Android operating system. According to this article, however, US regulators have generally declined to pursue antitrust remedies against US technology giants, preferring to let the market regulate competition.

How Russian Spies Hid Behind Bitcoin in Hacking Campaign

In the US Justice Department’s indictment of 12 Russian operatives accused of interfering with the 2016 presidential election, documents describe how the intelligence officers used the cryptocurrency Bitcoin to purchase tools that could be used in attacks on national security. According to sources quoted in the article, this is the first time such descriptions of the nefarious use of cryptocurrencies have been detailed in a court document.

Microsoft Says Hackers Already Targeted Three 2018 Midterm Candidates

A Microsoft executive recently disclosed that the company has detected multiple sophisticated attempts at cyber-attacks targeting three 2018 congressional candidates. This article reports that the attempts were linked to a group Microsoft calls “Strontium,” which is the same group of hackers with ties to Russian military intelligence who targeted the Democratic National Committee in 2016.

SEO News

Google Asks SEOs if Domain Changes Result in Loss of Traffic

John Mueller, a Webmaster Trends Analyst at Google, recently asked if changing domains resulted in a loss of web traffic. SEOs responded that, by and large, a domain change will result in a temporary loss of traffic. The consensus seemed to be that even with careful planning and closely following industry best practices, it is difficult for most sites to avoid a drop in traffic at least in the short term. Some advised waiting for a ‘slow season’ to make such a change, and planning the migration meticulously.

Google’s New Verification Process Lets Users Edit Knowledge Panels

For the first time, Google has allowed users to suggest edits to Knowledge Panels. Previously there was no way to alter the information in Knowledge Panels, which are boxes that often show up at the top of Google search results, and that pull information from sources across the web in an automated manner. Now, users who verify that they manage the online presence of the entity to whom the Knowledge Panel pertains can submit potential changes to what appears there.

Social Media News

MIT Research: How to Get More Twitter Followers

A recent paper published by MIT researchers provides insight into how to grow your Twitter follow count for free, with a conversion rate of up to 30 percent. The paper’s conclusions indicate that an account with a high follow count does not significantly gain more followers without taking additional steps, such as following and retweeting other users. This and other revelations were arrived at after researchers studied thousands of Twitter interactions and scenarios.

Big Twitter Accounts See Follower Numbers Drop After Fake-User Purge

Twitter users may have noticed that they lost followers in July following Twitter’s elimination of fake user accounts. Celebrities with a large number of followers noticed the most significant drops. Notably as well, Twitter’s own primary account, @Twitter, lost 12% of its follower count after the purge when it removed 7.5 million fake accounts that had been following it.

It’s Official: Brexit Campaign Broke the Law -- With Social Media’s Help

The results of an investigation into Brexit referendum spending are in. The UK’s Electoral Commission recently published its findings that the official “Vote Leave” campaign violated the law when it breached the set spending limits for election campaigns. This included the channeling of political advertising money to a data firm in Canada for the purposes of targeting Facebook users. “Vote Leave” also never declared that it was jointly working with “BeLeave,” another Brexit campaign. This is significant because “BeLeave” failed to disclose that it spent a substantial sum in conjunction with the Canadian data firm under a common arrangement with “Vote Leave.” The investigation concluded that this undisclosed spending may have tipped the campaigns over the maximum spending limit allowable under UK law.

Twitter Suspends Guccifer and DCLeaks After Mueller Links Them to Russian Hacking Operation

Twitter accounts Guccifer 2.0 and DCLeaks have been suspended by the social media company after special counsel Robert S. Mueller III linked them to a Russian intelligence operation that was designed to disrupt the 2016 US presidential election. The accounts were discovered to be digital fronts that Russian intelligence officers created for the purpose of laundering information hacked from Democrats’ computers. The US Department of Justice indicted a dozen of these officials on hacking charges in July. The charges illustrate how social media platforms worked into Russia’s disinformation campaign during the election and have left tech companies struggling to determine how best to prevent misuse of their networks.

Facebook to Remove Misinformation That Leads to Violence

Facebook will now remove any posts on its social network that contain false information which may lead to individuals being physically harmed. This is an expansion of Facebook’s existing rules about what type of misinformation it will remove. The decision comes after growing criticism surrounding posts that incited violence against ethnic minorities in countries such as Sri Lanka, Myanmar, and India after rumors were initially spread on social media.

Twitter Puts Verification Process Work on Hold to Focus on Elections Integrity

In an effort to focus on the health of its platform in advance of upcoming US elections, Twitter has put its Bluecheck/Verification process on hold. The product team at Twitter wants to focus on the quality of information shared on the social network as the US midterm elections approach this November. Once Twitter has made more progress in this area, it has indicated that it will take another look at addressing the Verification process.

Instagram Shows Users When Friends Are Online

A recent update to photo-sharing app Instagram now allows users to see when individuals they’ve direct messaged are online. The feature works in the same vein as Facebook Messenger, where a green dot will appear on a user’s profile picture, reflecting when they are active. This dot will also appear in the direct message inbox and on users’ friends lists. Only the online status of users who follow you back or those you have previously direct messaged will be visible.

Legal Industry News

Lawyer Gets Interim Suspension for Alleged ‘Copy and Paste’ Suits; Federal Judge Had Blasted Tactics

The Arizona State Bar has placed a Phoenix lawyer on interim suspension pending its investigation of his alleged practice of filing over 1,800 disability rights lawsuits against businesses in the state using essentially a ‘copy and paste’ template. The lawsuits reportedly contain false allegations, and the lawyer has since admitted that many of the individuals who did the investigative work for those legal actions were hired from Craigslist. The attorney had collected approximately $1.2 million in settlements in those cases, with most of that amount being comprised of attorney fees.

Courts Can’t Order Yelp to Take Down Negative Review of Law Firm, California Supreme Court Rules

The California Supreme Court has ruled that Yelp can’t be forced to remove a former client’s negative review of a law firm. Reversing the lower courts’ opinions, the state Supreme Court held that Yelp was protected under the federal Communications Decency Act, which generally shields entities that publish content by others from liability for defamation. The Court ruled that even though Yelp was a non-party in this case, the statute’s protections should extend to cover the popular review site.

Common Types of Encryption: What Lawyers Need to Know

Encryption offers lawyers a way to secure client data while it is in storage or in transit. While many lawyers are likely aware that they should be protecting client data, they may not know where to start if they are implementing it for the first time. This article provides a user-friendly breakdown of the types of encryption most likely to be relevant to a law practice, and the specific functions each one performs.

New Video From ABA President Calls for Lawyers to Help Separated Immigrant Families

ABA President Hilarie Bass has posted a short video describing what she saw and heard in a recent visit to the Port Isabel Detention Center in Texas, and asked lawyers to lend their services to help reunite immigrant families separated at the border. Bass met with immigrant mothers who hadn’t seen their children in weeks, and who were willing to voluntarily give up their asylum claims in order to have their children returned to them. Bass expressed concern that these parents were giving up legal rights to have their asylum cases heard and directed lawyers to a new ABA web page containing resources for those interested in assisting separated or detained families.

Ann Hopkins, Plaintiff in Landmark Gender Stereotyping Case, Dies at 74

Ann Hopkins, who was the plaintiff in the seminal 1989 Price Waterhouse v. Hopkins case, in which the Supreme Court ruled that Title VII of the Civil Rights Act covers discrimination against individuals who do not conform to gender norms, has died at the age of 74. Hopkins was famously denied partnership at Price Waterhouse based on her perceived failure to behave and dress in a more stereotypically feminine manner. Following her Supreme Court victory, Hopkins succeeded in proving that the firm had in fact discriminated against her, winning $370,000 in damages and a partnership. Hopkins worked at Price Waterhouse until her retirement in 2002.
