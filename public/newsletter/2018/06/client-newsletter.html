Tech News

California Net Neutrality Bill That AT&T Hates is Coming to New York, Too

In response to the FCC’s repeal of federal net neutrality rules, the California legislature is working on the strictest net neutrality law in the country, and New York is positioned to take the same route. Both state’s bills would prohibit internet service providers from blocking, throttling, or otherwise discriminating against content providers, and they would go a step further than the repealed federal rules by also banning paid data-cap exemptions.

A California Ruling Threatens the Gig Economy

The California Supreme Court ruled in Dynamex Operations West, Inc. v. Superior Court that in order for gig economy companies like Dynamex Operations West, Lyft, and Uber to classify their workers as contractors (as opposed to employees), the companies must prove that their workers are, in fact, running their own businesses. Under Dynamex, there is now a rebuttable presumption that a worker is an employee, and the employer must prove, under what is known as the “ABC test,” that the worker is properly classified as an independent contractor. This change will make it more difficult for some employers to classify their workers as independent contractors, as compared to the prior rule.

A Second State Moves Closer to Mandating Technology Training for Lawyers

In 2016, Florida became the first state to require that lawyers complete CLE in the use of technology. Now, North Carolina is poised to do the same. The North Carolina State Bar Council has approved a proposed amendment to lawyers’ annual CLE requirements that would mandate that one of the required 12 hours of CLE training cover technology.

Net Neutrality Rules Will End June 11 With The FCC's Final Say-So

The FCC’s net neutrality rules will be defunct on June 11, 2018. Repealed in December of last year, the rules prohibited internet service providers from throttling or blocking content providers who did not pay premium fees for the delivery of content. FCC Chairman Ajit Pai called the rules “heavy-handed, utility-style regulation” and is opting for a “light-touch” approach instead.

Supreme Court Declines to Hear “Podcasting Patent” Case, Handing Win to EFF

In a victory for podcasters everywhere, the US Supreme Court declined to hear a case in which a Texas company was seeking to enforce a patent that broadly described podcasts. The company had been sending letters to numerous podcasters demanding that they obtain a license to continue podcasting. The Electronic Frontier Foundation (EFF) challenged the underlying patent, and the US Court of Appeals for the Federal Circuit ruled in favor of the EFF on a central issue, allowing the challenge to proceed.

How a “Location API” Allows Cops to Figure Out Where We All Are in Real Time

The New York Times recently reported that a company used by jails and prisons to monitor prisoners’ calls also provides a lesser-known service that allows law enforcement officers to seek and obtain real-time location information about any mobile phone without a warrant. Courts are split on whether and when investigators need a warrant based on probable cause to acquire location data, but the technology is causing alarm not only among consumers, but with at least one US Senator who has called for additional information.

Uber Ends Policy of Forced Arbitration for Individual Sexual Assault Claims

Last month, ride sharing company Uber received a letter from a group of women who allegedly experienced sexual violence at the hands of Uber drivers. These women requested that they be released from the mandatory arbitration clause that appeared in the Uber app’s terms of service. As a result of this letter, Uber recently announced that it plans to end the required arbitration on individual claims of sexual assault, harassment, and violence by Uber drivers, riders, or employees, thus signaling a major change in policy for the company’s US operations.

SEO News

Google to Notify Those Who Leave Reviews When Business Owners Respond

Google will now be notifying Google Local customers and users that an owner has responded to their reviews. When a business or owner responds, reviewers will receive an email notification. This feature will also include a link to where the customer can view the owner’s full response. Google has plans to eventually add mobile push notifications, as well.

Google Confirms It Shortened Search Results Snippets After Expanding Them Last December

Mid-May signaled several updates and improvements on Google-related products, as well as other SEO-centric news. After expanding search results snippets last December, Google confirmed in May that it decided to shorten them again. Google also added a feature to its Google Local services that allows users to be notified when a business responds to their reviews, and the Google Blog published a post describing a simple way to get the most out of its search engine.

Google Asking Dissatisfied Searchers to Submit Questions Manually in the Search Results

Google has confirmed that it is testing a new search results feature. If searchers are unable to find the answer they are seeking, Google will be asking them to submit more details about their specific question directly to the company in a search results box. Once submitted, the information will be fed to Google and may then get passed on to content creators. The goal is to encourage these content creators to research and publish articles onto their web properties that answer these questions so future searchers will then find the answer they are looking for in the results of Google’s search engine.

Google Posts Now Accepting Videos for Local Businesses

Google Posts is a feature that enables businesses and individuals to create content on Google directly. It will then appear as a highly ranked result in Google’s search engine when people search for these brands and names. In the past, Google Posts has only allowed users to upload photos. Now, Google has launched a new feature that supports the upload of videos, giving these entities an additional option by which to create highly ranked content.

How to Survive Google’s New Local Search World

In light of the many recent changes to Google Local Search, this article describes some of the most important changes and shares tips on how to keep your local business visible in the search results. Among the tips are: optimize your business’s knowledge graph, actively manage your Google My Business listing, and test your search results.

Social Media News

Facebook Faces Fresh Criticism Over Ad Targeting of Sensitive Interests

According to an investigation by the Guardian and Danish Broadcasting Corporation, Facebook allows advertisers to target its users based on categories, such as political beliefs, sexuality, and religion. These are considered sensitive pieces of information that fall under the purview of European data protection law. This includes the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), which applies across the European Union as of May 25. Since Facebook has not asked its users for explicit consent before it makes sensitive inferences of this nature, some lawmakers believe it may be running afoul of these European regulations.

Twitter Algorithm Changes Will Hide More Bad Tweets and Trolls

Twitter is making changes that aim to counter trolling and abuse on its platform. Using its algorithms, tweets in Twitter conversations and searches will now be ranked based on a large wealth of data that considers details such as the number of accounts registered to the specific user and whether their tweet prompted other users to block those accounts. These tweets will be deemed more likely to be bad or coming from a troll account. While the tweets won’t be automatically deleted, they’ll be pushed down into a “Show more replies” section where they’ll be seen by fewer other users.

Facebook Introduces Voice Posts, a Stories Archive, and Cloud Storage

Facebook has announced three new features that will allow users to make voice-only posts, keep an archive of stories past their standard 24-hour lifespan, and store photos and video taken with the in-app camera within Facebook rather than on their own device. These changes will be available in India first, and then will be extended to other countries.

Legal Industry News

Smart Compose and Offline Mode Have Arrived in Gmail

For those using the latest version of Gmail, Google has announced two new features that may facilitate more flexibility in tracking and managing emails. First, offline mode is available to account holders who also use Chrome as their web browser. This feature will allow users to read email, archive it, and perform other functions while not connected to the internet. Second, Gmail users will soon be able to take advantage of an AI-powered predictive text feature that will autocomplete entire phrases and full sentences.

Judge Sanctions Lawyer After Discovery of Instagram Photos Exposing Family Emergency Excuse

A New Jersey federal court has fined an attorney $10,000 following revelations that she made misrepresentations to the court regarding a family emergency that she said caused her to miss a filing deadline. Opposing counsel submitted posts to the court from the lawyer’s Instagram account that appeared to contradict the information she had filed with the court regarding her emergency travel. The attorney later admitted that she had not been honest with the court and withdrew from the case.

Lawyers Weigh in: Why is There a Depression Epidemic in the Profession?

Statistics show that lawyers are 3.6 times more likely to be suffering from depression as people in other jobs, with 28% of employed lawyers being affected. Other estimates show that 19% of working attorneys have symptoms of anxiety, and 21% have issues with alcohol abuse. In this article, lawyers from across the spectrum of practice areas and career experience offer their thoughts as to why these issues are so prevalent among members of the legal profession.

Biglaw Firms Become Tech Incubators

Three large UK-based law firms are looking to foster the development of cutting-edge legal technology by sponsoring collaborative efforts between startups and the firms themselves. By offering tech incubation spaces, opportunities to work alongside their lawyers, and in some cases funding and legal support, the firms are seeking to pave the way for a new wave of tech innovation within the legal industry.

Is Your Legal Tech Initiative Working?

A critical aspect of implementing any new technology in your law practice is measuring its impact and effectiveness. Using data to track objectives, outcomes, losses, and lessons from these initiatives can help lawyers project costs, plan for the future, and see the bigger picture associated with integrating any new technology.

Top Law Schools Ask Law Firms to Disclose Arbitration Agreements For Summer Associates

Fourteen of the country’s top law schools will now require law firms participating in on-campus recruiting to disclose in advance whether students they hire will be subject to mandatory arbitration agreements. The new rule was established after student organizers argued that law schools, rather than students, should be responsible for asking firms if new hires would be subject to such agreements. Following the release of a new survey from the law schools requiring employers to be transparent about the existence of such policies, multiple large firms have announced the elimination of mandatory arbitration agreements for summer associates.
