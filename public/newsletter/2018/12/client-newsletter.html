TIP OF THE MONTH

Stay Focused on Your Legal Website - Beware of Fake Auto-generated Audits

You may receive solicitations from third parties with recommendations for your website based on "audits" they have performed. Please note that many of these are generic statements with no actual examples of issues about your website. Others point at issues that do not exist, or in fact are wrong (e.g. suggesting to use schema type "attorney" rather than "legal services" when the schema type "attorney" has been explicitly been marked for removal on the site schema.org used by Google; see https://schema.org/Attorney). Many of them also misinterpret some of the technical aspects and report errors where there are none (e.g. their checkers cannot read the JSON-LD text). In any case, if you do have a question about a recommendation, please feel free to forward it to us for review.

Please note that we systematically check your site and run customized audits each month to generate your SEO report. We recommend checking your Justia SEO report and following your customized recommendations which your account manager specifically provides to you to best optimize your web presence.

Tech News

Amazon’s Alexa Might Be a Key Witness in a Murder Case

A New Hampshire judge has ordered Amazon to turn over data from an Echo smart speaker that was present in a home where a double murder took place. This request highlights an emerging trend of law enforcement requests for user data collected by smart speakers, Fitbits, iPhones, and other Internet-connected devices in criminal cases.

What Happens to Free Speech When We All Live in a Virtual World?

The conservative co-founder of virtual reality company Oculus is more concerned about threats to free speech than the risks posed by artificial intelligence or facial recognition technology. Envisioning a future where people spend most of their time in a virtual world, he worries that people will be restricted in the exchange of ideas and lack an understanding of the concept of free speech in that setting.

FCC Tells SpaceX It Can Deploy Up to 11,943 Broadband Satellites

The FCC has granted SpaceX approval to deploy over 7,500 broadband satellites in addition to the almost 4,500 satellites that were approved for the company earlier this year. The larger group of satellites that was recently approved will operate at a low orbit close to the Earth, which SpaceX says will improve broadband performance in highly populated areas. The FCC has conditioned its approval of the additional satellites on SpaceX providing a satisfactory space debris mitigation plan in light of the large number of devices it proposes to release into orbit.

Supreme Court Rejects Industry Challenge of 2015 Net Neutrality Rules

The US Supreme Court will not hear the broadband industry’s challenge to the Obama administration’s net neutrality rules. These rules were already reversed by FCC Chairman Ajit Pai, who was appointed under the Trump administration. However, broadband companies and lobbyists were attempting to further undermine the 2015 Obama rules by undoing court precedent supporting them.

Court Filings Accidentally Reveal Charges Against Julian Assange

A filing in Virginia federal court seems to have inadvertently revealed that the federal government has initiated charges against WikiLeaks founder Julian Assange. It is not entirely clear what he may have been charged with, though charges may potentially be related to WikiLeaks’ release of hacked emails from the Democratic Party during the 2016 presidential campaign. Assange continues to stay at the Ecuadorian embassy in London, where he was granted asylum several years ago.

Feds Using ‘Reverse Warrants’ to Gather Location/Identifying Info on Thousands of Non-Suspects

Recent reports indicate that the FBI has been attempting to obtain data from Google regarding the location histories of any individuals with smart devices that were in the area of crime scenes at the times that crimes were committed. While courts have in some cases agreed to issue warrants of this nature, Google seems to be pushing back and declining to release the requested information in some cases in light of the arguably overbroad nature of these requests.

Apple, Facebook, and Google Among 56 Businesses Telling Trump Not to Weaken Transgender Rights

Tech giants and other companies have once again joined forces to oppose the Trump administration’s attempts to roll back protections for transgender individuals, including a US Department of Health and Human Services proposal to define a person’s gender under Title IX as immutable trait based on their genitals at birth. Google, Facebook, Amazon, and dozens of other businesses wrote a letter to the president, calling this sort of policy discriminatory and harmful to their companies because it threatened to undermine civil rights protections for transgender people.

Neo-Nazis Have No First Amendment Right to Harassment, Judge Rules

A federal judge in Montana has ruled that the First Amendment will not provide legal protection for the owner of a neo-Nazi website being sued in civil court for an alleged campaign of online harassment against a Jewish real estate agent and her family, friends, and colleagues. Ruling that the real estate agent was a private citizen and not a public figure, the judge stated that the publisher encouraged his supporters to harass her through their campaign, which resulted in the family receiving over 700 threatening messages.

Amazon Chooses Queens and a Washington Suburb for ‘Second Headquarters’

After a long and competitive process involving bids from many metropolitan areas seeking to boost their presence in the tech economy, Amazon has announced that rather than choosing a single location for its second headquarters, it will have new ‘headquarters’ offices in Queens, New York and the Arlington area in Virginia. The company will plan to have approximately 50,000 workers in those locations, where some local residents worry about the housing, traffic, and tax subsidy impacts.

Google and Facebook Back Berners-Lee’s Case #ForTheWeb

Internet pioneer Tim Berners-Lee has has called on companies and individuals to sign a Contract for the Web and pledge to make internet access affordable, accessible, safe, and empowering for everyone. Major tech companies such as Google and Facebook have become signatories, though Berners-Lee is also interested in attracting a diverse group of supporters, specifically smaller entities and women.

SEO News

Search Engines Still Dominate Over Social Media, Even For Millennials

Despite the rise of social media usage, particularly among users 18-34 years old, all age groups still turn to search engines as their go-to source of information. Map apps are also a popular choice for looking up businesses and addresses, while voice search has yet to become a favored option among most users.

Google Maps Now Lets Users Send Messages to Businesses

In addition to offering this option in Google My Business, Google will now permit users of its Maps app to message business owners directly through the app. The messages show up as SMS messages, and business owners who use this feature will be able to track stats such as the number of clicks on the message button over time. Businesses can also opt out of using this feature.

Chrome 71 Will Block Any and All Ads On Sites With “Abusive Experiences”

Google Chrome version 71 will offer a feature that evaluates sites for what Google describes as “abusive experiences,” with the aim of blacklisting sites that are deemed repeat offenders. The consequences of being blacklisted will mean the suppression of all advertising on qualifying sites. According to Google, abusive experiences include ads that appear as system error messages, fake close boxes that bounce users to the advertising site, and any type of phishing scheme or malware. Chrome 71 is set to release in December.

Google Showing Fewer Videos in Their Search Results

According to two analytics tools, Google’s search results are showing approximately 30% fewer video boxes and carousels than were shown just a few weeks ago. While single videos still remain in desktop search results, no such replacements are shown in mobile results. At this time, it remains unclear if this is a bug or a permanent change to how Google intends to show videos.

Social Media News

Facebook Drops PR Firm After Revelation of Anti-Soros Campaign

In mid-November 2018, the New York Times revealed that Facebook has chosen to sever ties with Definers, a conservative public relations group. This decision comes after Facebook discovered that the group had shared a document linking left-wing Facebook critics to George Soros. According to the Times, this document cast Soros as the leading force behind an expansive anti-Facebook movement. While Soros has made donations to various liberal groups via his Open Society Foundation, he states that no grants have been made specifically to support campaigns against Facebook. The misleading document that Definers shared appears to be a driving force behind Facebook’s decision no longer to work with Definers.

Facebook Joins Google, Halts Mandatory Arbitration in Sexual Harassment Cases

Following policy changes by Google, Microsoft, and Uber, Facebook recently announced to its employees that its own arbitration policy would be undergoing changes. Facebook will no longer require mandatory arbitration when an employee files a sexual harassment claim. In addition, the Wall Street Journal noted that Facebook has also updated its policy associated with interoffice dating. Executives at or above the director level must now disclose whether they are dating another Facebook employee.

Now Eight Parliaments Are Demanding Zuckerberg Answers for Facebook Scandals

Pressure is mounting for Facebook founder Mark Zuckerberg to accept the invitation of eight international parliaments whose lawmakers want to ask him about how Facebook has negatively impacted democratic processes across the globe. The questions relate to recent instances of data misuse and security breaches on Facebook’s platform. Facebook already declined an invitation from five of these parliaments in November, which included a grand committee led by the UK representing the interests of Facebook users in Argentina, Australia, Canada, Ireland, and the UK The request has since been reissued with the addition of parliaments from Brazil, Latvia, and Singapore.

Facebook Reports a Massive Spike in Government Demands for Data, Including Secret Orders

Facebook recently released details about 13 letters related to national security that it received between 2014 and 2017. The letters were effectively subpoenas, requesting user data for several Facebook and Instagram accounts. The letters are considered controversial because they usually arrive with an order preventing social media companies from reaching out to the subjects of the letter. They often include orders for IP addresses of individuals with whom the subject has corresponded, online purchases the user has made, and cell-site location data, among other things. Over the course of this year, the government decided to lift its gag orders, which is why Facebook is now allowed to publish the details without the threat of legal reprisal.

Facebook Fallout Ruptures Democrats’ Longtime Alliance With Silicon Valley

Since the discovery that social media platforms like Facebook and Twitter allowed hate speech, Russian propaganda, and conservative-friendly "fake news" to spread without much oversight, a rift has grown between Democrats and Silicon Valley. Tensions recently increased after the New York Times reported that executives at Facebook withheld knowledge of Russian activity for longer than originally believed while at the same time employing a Republican-supporting public relations firm whose aim was to discredit liberal-leaning critics. These revelations have reinforced criticism from progressive government officials, as well as complicated life for those who remain allied to tech firms.

Legal Industry News

Cybersecurity and the Realities of Practicing Law in 2018

Cybersecurity for lawyers was the guiding theme at this year’s Futures Conference in Boston, Massachusetts. It was an especially timely topic, due to recent data breaches and troubling statistics which reveal that a third of law firms with between 10 and 99 attorneys were affected by cyber breaches in 2017. Conference speakers revealed other eye-opening information, including that email phishing and other scams are often among the most successful hacking tactics directed at law firms. A large takeaway from the conference was the importance of exercising caution. It is no longer a viable option to ignore the realities of cybersecurity, as no law firm is immune without taking adequate steps to protect itself against breaches of this nature.

ABA Announces Hotlines for California Fire Victims; State Bar Warns of Fraud as First Suits Filed

Victims of California’s most recent string of wildfires now have legal hotlines they can call, thanks to the joint efforts of the American Bar Association Young Lawyers Division, the Federal Emergency Management Agency, the State Bar of California, Legal Services of Northern California, and Ventura County Legal Aid. Hotlines are available for fire victims in Butte, Ventura, and Los Angeles counties. Qualifying individuals who call in are matched with attorneys who have volunteered their time to help with landlord/tenant legal problems, mortgage or foreclosure issues, insurance and FEMA claims, and more.

Lowest Bar Pass Rates for California in 67 Years; Other States See Drop, Too

California’s July 2018 bar passage rate dropped almost nine percentage points from the July 2017 rate, making it the lowest bar passage rate in 67 years. Many reasons have been considered for the steep drop, including more law schools admitting students with below-average LSAT scores and college GPAs, a lack of adequate preparation for the bar itself, and the fact that new law graduates are young enough that they weren’t raised on an education with as much reliance on rote memorization as past generations. Others within the legal profession believe the answer lies in constructing a licensing exam that better reflects the skills required to practice law in the real world.

Dean Chemerinsky Recommends Removing Boalt Name From Use At Berkeley Law

A committee assembled by Berkeley Law’s dean, Erwin Chemerinsky, has recommended removing the surname of John Boalt from its law building and disassociating the name from all campus groups due to its racist implications. The name comes from nineteenth century mining industry baron and Nevada attorney John Boalt, who held racist views that Chinese people are inferior to white Americans. Boalt also espoused negative opinions about African Americans and Native Americans. While the Boalt name is merely honorific and has never been officially part of the law school’s name, the building in which the law school is housed bears the Boalt name and the law school is known to many as the same. It will be up to Berkeley’s chancellor to make the final decision about removing Boalt’s name from the building.

Judge’s Facebook Friendship With Lawyer Doesn’t Necessarily Require Recusal, Says Florida Top Court

Connections or friendships with lawyers on Facebook don’t default disqualify judges from hearing cases in which those lawyers represent one of the parties, according to the Florida Supreme Court. In a split 4-3 decision, the court reasoned that a Facebook friendship is less of a traditional friendship than a digital connection. Friendships on social media tend to be more casual than those offline. The court held that without more evidence of a stronger offline bond, proof of a Facebook friendship does not in itself indicate a party will receive an unfair or partial trial, or necessitate that a judge recluse themself from hearing a particular case in Florida.

Kavanaugh Keeps Low Profile Since His Confirmation, Breaks With Conservatives in a Few Votes

Since his Supreme Court confirmation hearing, Justice Brett Kavanaugh seems to be trying to avoid attracting overt attention, as noted by CNN. Many times, he has opted to defer to other justices during oral arguments, allowing his colleagues to ask questions first. When he does ask questions, Justice Kavanaugh asks them of both sides of a case, which has led some news outlets to speculate that he may be attempting to avoid typical conservative labels for the time being. CNN also noted that the other justices seem to be taking care to appear openly welcoming to Justice Kavanaugh, showing “an unusual degree of collegiality all around during arguments so far this term.”
