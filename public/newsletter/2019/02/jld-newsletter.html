Tech News

Board Sued Over Google’s Exit Package for Accused Executive

A shareholder of Google’s parent company, Alphabet, has filed a lawsuit in the San Mateo County Superior Court against the board of directors over the substantial exit package it gave to former senior executive Andy Rubin, who was credibly accused of sexual harassment. The lawsuit alleges that the board played an “active and direct role” in approving the $90 million severance and that it was intended to “keep the matter quiet.” The lawsuit seeks damages and policy changes to improve oversight and transparency and to improve corporate governance.

Feds Forcing Mass Fingerprint Unlocks is an “Abuse of Power,” Judge Rules

A federal magistrate judge in California recently ruled that police cannot use a warrant issued for searching a criminal suspect’s house to also seize all digital devices and compel anyone present to unlock those devices biometrically. Magistrate Judge Kandis Westmore pointed out that the government had other legal means to obtain digital data, and “[w]hile it may be more expedient to circumvent Facebook and attempt to gain access by infringing on the Fifth Amendment's privilege against self-incrimination, it is an abuse of power and is unconstitutional.”

Software Patents Poised to Make a Comeback Under New Patent Office Rules

The US Patent and Trademark Office recently published revisions to its Manual of Patent Examination Procedure (MPEP), which offers guidance to patent examiners. The revision reportedly includes far tighter restrictions on what may be excluded from patentability, leading critics to express concerns that the new policies could make it too easy for so-called patent trolls to get broad software patents—and to sue companies that accidentally infringe them.

Court: Politicians Who Block Citizens on Social Media Violate 1st Amendment

The US Court of Appeals for the Fourth Circuit recently held, in Davison v. Randall, that government officials cannot block citizens from posting on official social media accounts without running afoul of the First Amendment of the US Constitution. The judge ruled that the First Amendment limits the government’s ability to censor speech in a public forum, such as a governmental official page on Facebook.

That GoFundMe to Build a Border Wall Is Issuing $20 Million In Refunds

A GoFundMe campaign that raised $20 million in an attempt to help the Trump administration build a wall on the southern US border has run into problems with its fundraising platform. The original language of the campaign promised donors a refund if the $1 billion goal was not met, but that language reportedly disappeared in December, in violation of GoFundMe policies. GoFundMe will automatically refund all the donations, and anyone who still wants their money to go to the campaign will need to affirmatively opt in.

Cambridge Analytica’s Parent Pleads Guilty to Breaking UK Data Law

The parent company of Cambridge Analytica, the political analytics firm accused of mishandling massive amounts of Facebook data around the 2016 presidential election, has pleaded guilty to breaking UK law and received a fine of £15,000. In an official statement, UK Information Commissioner Elizabeth Denham said, “This prosecution, the first against Cambridge Analytica, is a warning that there are consequences for ignoring the law. Wherever you live in the world, if your data is being processed by a UK company, UK data protection laws apply.”

Despite Promises to Stop, US Cell Carriers Are Still Selling Your Real-Time Phone Location Data

In 2017, four of the largest US cell carriers (AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile, and Sprint) were discovered to be selling real-time location data of their customers to third parties, who could then locate any phone using only the phone number. Despite the substantial criticism directed at the carriers, they allegedly are still selling the data, albeit to a different third party (that offers the same service).

FCC Asks Court for Delay in Case That Could Restore Net Neutrality Rules

In the court case challenging the Federal Communications Commission (FCC’s) decision at the end of December 2017 to repeal net neutrality rules, the FCC recently asked the court to delay oral arguments due to the government shutdown. The court denied the FCC’s request, so oral arguments will take place as scheduled on February 1.

SEO News

Google Search Console Makes ‘Inspect URL’ Tool More Useful

Google’s latest update to its Search Console includes information about HTTP responses codes, JavaScript logs, and rendered screenshots, allowing users to access even more information about how their site is performing from an SEO standpoint. After a user logs into Search Console, they can access this information via navigating to the Inspect URL tool, clicking on “view crawled page,” then clicking on different tabs that display the new sets of data from a right-hand sidebar.

Google Ordered to Submit Search Index to State Sponsorship in Russia

Roskomnadzor, a Russian information agency, is requiring search engines like Google and Bing to submit their search index results for government censorship. A recent Russian law requires search engine results to be filtered through FGIS (the federal state information system). This new Russian law is similar to rules imposed by China that require internet companies to censor results, plus block information the country has deemed undesirable or threatening. Failure to comply with the filtering law incurs monetary fines in Russia.

Google May be Headed for Victory in EU Right-To-Be-Forgotten Indexing Appeal

It appears that Google could win its appeal of a 2015 decision pitting it against French data-protection authority CNIL (Commission National de l‘informatique et des Libertés). Initially, CNIL had demanded a global delisting of data from Google’s EU index under the European Union’s “right to be forgotten” (RTBF) law. Recently, however, the Court Justice of the European Union’s top legal advisor issued an opinion that agrees with Google, taking the position that the search company should not be required to enforce RTBF beyond the EU. While the case has yet to be officially decided, it has global significance and any decision would apply equally to Bing and other search engines.

New Study Reveals What People Are Buying Through Voice Assistants

OnBuy.com recently surveyed US consumers to see what products and services they purchased using voice assistants. The study analyzed findings from ‘VoiceBot.AI,’ which polled a little over 1,200 US consumers. The study also identified consumers’ largest positive preferences and their greatest concerns about voice shopping. Everyday household items came out on top as the products consumers most often buy using voice assistants.

Bing Ads to Exclusively Serve Yahoo Traffic Starting in March

Microsoft Bing Ads recently announced an expanded partnership with Verizon Media at its annual Bing Ads Next web conference. The partnership would enable Bing Ads to exclusively serve Yahoo search traffic, including traffic from the Oath Ad Platforms (formerly known as Yahoo Gemini), plus other partners. Bing Ads, Yahoo, and AOL will soon all be simplified and able to be accessed under the Bing Ads umbrella. Users will also be able to manage them all in the same Bing Ads platform.

Google Shares Tips For Success in Google News Search Results

Danny Sullivan, Google’s Public Liaison for Search, recently published a post on the Google Webmaster blog offering best practices and advice to publishers who submit their site for inclusion in Google News. Sullivan reminded publishers about the Google News Publisher Help Center and provided advice on duplicate content, transparency in article bylines, and how to make headlines clear and accurate. By and large, everything Sullivan noted is considered already established best practices, but the post serves as a solid refresher at the start of a new year.

Social Media News

Older People Shared Fake News on Facebook More Than Others in 2016 Race, Study Says

According to a new study published by Wednesday in Science Advances, Facebook users age 65 and older shared fake news at the highest percentage during the 2016 United States presidential election. The study illustrates how aggressively seniors spread false information on Facebook. On average, US Facebook users who were 65 years old and older posted seven times as many fake news website articles as users aged 29 and younger, per the study. This finding remained true across ideologies, educational levels, and political affiliation. Despite this revelation, the study also indicated that sharing news articles from disreputable sources was relatively uncommon on the whole.

Instagram Now Lets You Regram Your Posts to Multiple Accounts

Photo-sharing social network Instagram recently added the ability to publish feed posts to multiple accounts at the same time by simply toggling them on within the IG composer screen. Instagram is rolling out this option to all iOS users to “provide a better experience for people who often post to multiple accounts.”

Zuckerberg’s 2019 Challenge Is To Hold Public Talks on Tech & Society

While Facebook creator Mark Zuckerberg’s focus for 2018 was largely internal, he recently announced that his 2019 challenge would shift to focusing on external discussions about the role of technology in society in the future. He indicated that he plans to hold talks with different leaders, experts, and community members and that these talks would encompass a range of formats and venues. The one guiding similarity: they will all be publicly viewable from Zuckerberg’s Facebook and Instagram accounts or other traditional media.

10 Reasons Why Social Media is Important to Your Company

Though often seen as an afterthought to a brand’s digital marketing efforts, social media can play a key role in increasing a business’ online presence and overall growth. In addition to being a tool for reputation management, PR, and SEO, social media marketing can help a brand in reaching new prospective clients and recruiting employees.

Despite Controversies, Facebook Apps Were the Most Used and Downloaded in 2018

App Annie named Facebook the most used app of 2018, despite its troubling year marked by data breaches and privacy concerns. In addition, Facebook-owned apps Messenger, WhatsApp Messenger, and Instagram were among the most used and downloaded.

YouTube Is Ramping Up Enforcement of Its Community Guidelines

In response to community feedback, YouTube is strengthening enforcement for violations of its policies related to custom thumbnails containing adult content or extreme violence, external links to adult content or spam, and dangerous challenges and pranks. The company will initially issue strikes to account holders who break rules in these areas, and in some cases three strikes in 90 days will lead to account termination.

Legal Industry News

How to Redact a PDF and Protect Your Clients

The recent failure of Paul Manafort’s lawyers to properly redact a court filing, resulting in confidential information being made public, serves as a reminder to lawyers of the importance of correctly using redacting functions in electronic documents. This article provides a how-to guide and troubleshooting tips for attorneys seeking to protect client data in e-filings and other documents.

Rather Than Increasing 1L Class Size In 2018, Some Law Schools Shrunk Strategically

Although 2018 was the first year since 2010 that has seen a significant increase in the number of law school applicants, a handful of law schools have reduced incoming class sizes. In a bid for higher law school rankings, these schools are seeking to increase their median LSAT scores, raise bar passage rates and improve employment outcomes by pursuing a more selective student body.

Judge Penalizes Lawyers for Not Using Artificial Intelligence

A judge in Canada has admonished a team of lawyers and reduced the award for attorney fees and costs that they were seeking, in part because they failed to use artificial intelligence in conducting legal research. Although the opinion is vague as to the type of artificial intelligence the judge had in mind or what the specific cost benefits would have been, it provides a possible preview of the standards to which attorneys will likely be held in the future with regard to use of legal technology that can improve efficiency and professional services.

How to Unlock a Firm’s Data Potential

While law firms generally collect and house enormous amounts of information as a routine matter, few have taken the necessary steps to utilize all of that unstructured data in a strategic manner. By designing a comprehensive and agile approach to internal data collection, analysis, and deployment, law firms can increase operations efficiency, improve client experiences, and better predict litigation and other outcomes.

Six Strategies to Safeguard Your Mobile Data

With increasing numbers of attorneys working on the go or outside of traditional brick-and-mortar offices, data security in mobile devices has become more important than ever. Steps that lawyers can take to protect client confidences and other sensitive information communicated via smartphones, tablets, and laptops include encrypting data, using two-factor authentication, and consulting with data security professionals.
