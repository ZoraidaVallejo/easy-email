Tech News

Democrats Introduce Save the Internet Act to Restore Net Neutrality

Democrats in both the US Senate and the House introduced the Save the Internet Act of 2019, which would restore the open internet rules that the FCC repealed in 2017. Promoting what is known as “net neutrality,” the law would prevent internet service providers from blocking or slowing access to certain types of content and from charging content providers fees for access to consumers.

AG Barr Invokes States Secrets Privileges in Twitter Suit

As readers may recall, Twitter sought to publish a transparency report describing the amount of national security legal process the company received in 2013 in response to allegedly misleading (or incomplete) statements by the Department of Justice about the matter. The DOJ declined Twitter’s request to publish the information, citing national security, and Twitter sued under the First Amendment. In a recent development, Attorney General Barr invoked the state secrets privilege to support its continued withholding of the information.

How Google Influences the Conversation in Washington

Ars Technica reports on Google’s lobbying efforts in Washington, DC, describing it as “shift[ing] into overdrive” as legislators are increasingly scrutinizing the company and other technology companies. This article describes some of the efforts undertaken by Google to influence policy.

Google Paid $105 Million to Two Executives Accused of Sexual Harassment

Google is reportedly facing criticism in the form of a shareholder lawsuit for paying $105 million in exit packages for two executives who had been accused of sexual harassment. Filed by shareholder James Martin, the lawsuit alleges that Alphabet (Google’s parent company) “active[ly] and direct[ly]” participated in a “multi-year scheme to cover up sexual harassment and discrimination” within the company.

‘Breathalyzer for Texting’: Nevada Weighs Controversial Technology to Curb Distracted Driving

Nevada is the latest state to consider allowing police to examine the cell phones of drivers to determine whether they were using a mobile device at or near the time of a car crash. Dubbed a “textalyzer,” this device would scan for recent activity when connected to a cell phone. Currently New York, New Jersey, and the city of Chicago, Illinois are also debating similar legislation. Backers of the measure claim that current distracted driving laws are difficult to enforce. However, privacy advocates, such as the ACLU, have raised concerns about the types of data textalyzers would be able to collect, as well as the use of the device without a warrant.

SEO News

Google Has Quietly Added DuckDuckGo as a Search Engine Option for Users in Over 60 Markets

Without apparent fanfare, Google recently updated its Chrome browser to expand the list of default search engines offered to users in over 60 global markets. The changes were pushed out in the mid-March Chromium 73 stable release. These per-country search engine choices seem to line up approximately with the top-four search engines by market share.

Report: 65 Million US Smart Speaker Owners, Smart Displays Quickly Gaining Traction

Voicebot.ai and Edison Research have recently released reports on smart speaker ownership in the United States. Both surveys estimate that around 65 million smart speaker owners have, on average, two devices per household, with Amazon and Google leading the US smart speaker market. The most popular location for a smart speaker was the living room. Voicebot.ai surveyed US adults age 18 and older while Edison Research focused on the US population 12 and older.

Google Took Down 2.3 Billion Bad Ads, 1 Million Ad Accounts in 2018

Google reported that it took down 2.3 billion “bad” ads for policy violations on its platform in 2018. While this is approximately a million fewer than Google removed back in 2017, the company indicated that it terminated almost double the number of advertiser accounts in 2018 from the previous year. Some of the primary sectors Google aimed its focus on last year were local paid services such as home repair, for-profit bail bond services, third-party tech support, and cryptocurrency.

Social Media News

Mark Zuckerberg's 6 New Principles of Privacy and Safety For Facebook and Messaging

In early March, Facebook’s CEO, Mark Zuckerberg, published a three thousand word note on his personal Facebook account, sharing his thoughts on internet privacy and how six key principles aligned with his social networking privacy-focused vision. While these principles won’t immediately appear on Facebook and its associated products, Zuckerberg indicated that they will serve as a roadmap for product development in the coming years. The principles relate to private interaction, encryption and safety, reducing permanence, safety, interoperability, and secure data storage.

Facebook Settles ACLU Job Advertisement Discrimination Lawsuit

Facebook has settled a lawsuit brought against it by the ACLU, among others, claiming that the social media company illegally permitted employers to target job applicants based on categories including race, sex, and age. Facebook has stated that it is implementing widespread changes to its job advertising platform in response to the civil rights audit it conducted following the lawsuit, and has engaged a civil rights law firm to evaluate its ads tools.

Google+ Public Posts to be Preserved by the Internet Archive Wayback Machine

With the shut-down of Google+ imminent, Internet Archive has announced that Google+’s public posts will be preserved via the Wayback Machine. Private posts, previously deleted content, and full-resolution photo or video content will not be saved.

Legal Industry News

Chief Justice Considering Possibility of Ethics Code for Supreme Court, Kagan Reveals

While testifying before a House of Representatives appropriations subcommittee regarding the Supreme Court’s budget, Justice Elena Kagan disclosed that Chief Justice John Roberts is contemplating creating a separate code of judicial ethics that would apply only to the high court. Kagan’s statement came in response to discussion regarding accountability for judges in the #MeToo era.

Can Judges and Law Clerks Participate in Educational Seminars? Partisanship is a Factor, Opinion Says

The Judicial Conference of the United States has authorized its Committee on Codes of Conduct to publish an advisory opinion stating that federal judges and law clerks must consider the partisan affiliation and activities of any group sponsoring an educational seminar they plan to attend. The opinion comes on the heels of criticism by a number of Democratic senators regarding the conservative Heritage Foundation’s role in providing a training camp for judicial clerks.
