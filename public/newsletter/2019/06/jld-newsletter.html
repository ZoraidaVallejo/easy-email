Tech News

Student Files $1 Billion Lawsuit Against Apple Over Supposedly Faulty Facial Recognition Tech That Falsely Accused Him of Theft

A student in New York has filed a lawsuit against Apple alleging that the facial recognition technology the company uses as part of its stores’ security systems erroneously identified him as the perpetrator of numerous thefts from Apple Stores across several states. The lawsuit alleges that Apple’s conduct constitutes negligence, intentional infliction of emotional distress, negligent infliction of emotional distress, defamation, slander, libel, and fraudulent concealment and requests $1 billion in damages.

Refunds for 300 Million Phone Users Sought in Lawsuits Over Location Data Sales

Attorneys filed lawsuits against each of the major U.S. wireless carriers—AT&T, Verizon, T-Mobile, and Sprint—alleging that they sold customers’ real-time location data to third parties, in violation of federal law. The lawsuits seek class action status and request monetary damages “in an amount to be proven at trial.”

Supreme Court Ruling Could Threaten Apple’s 30 Percent App Commission

In Apple v. Pepper, the U.S. Supreme Court held, by a 5-4 majority, that a group of consumers may proceed with an antitrust lawsuit against Apple alleging that its operation of the App Store is unlawful monopolization of the market. The case did not reach the merits of whether Apple's behavior constitutes an unlawful monopoly, only that the iPhone owners may file the suit, even though app developers are the more directly injured parties of Apple’s alleged monopolistic conduct.

Trump Wants to Wall Off Huawei, but the Digital World Bridles at Barriers

The ongoing conflict between the United States and Chinese phone manufacturer Huawei may be culminating in the exclusion of the company from all American-made technology. In practical terms that may prove critical, new Huawei phones will come without Google apps and American computer chip companies will not supply Huawei with the chips needed to work on 5G wireless networks.

SEO News

Here’s What You Need to Know About Image Optimization for SEO

Millions of users discover images and websites using Google Image Search. When a recent change allowed users to more easily jump from image results to the website where the image is found, images became a more important source of website traffic. This article summarizes some of the ways you can optimize images on your website and attract more traffic, including adding alt and title attributes to your images.

Google Shows Off the New Speed Report Coming to Search Console

Google announced during its I/O developer conference that it will be unrolling a new page speed performance report in Search Console. In conjunction with this official announcement, Google released screenshots of the new report on desktop and mobile.

Google to Improve Search Experience for News and Podcasts

Google CEO Sundar Pichai also announced that Google will be adding its “Full Coverage” feature to its search result pages. This feature first appeared in Google News, and was used to show the range of relevant news stories available from multiple outlets when a user was reading breaking news. Google will also be transcribing podcasts so that they can be indexed and searchable.

Mobile, Desktop Searches May Have Stabilized at Roughly 60% - 40%

Search Engine Land reports on a study revealing that 65% of internet advertising revenue comes from mobile, compared to 35% from desktop. Additionally, it appears that the split between mobile and desktop searches has stabilized, with 60% of searches coming from mobile devices and 40% coming from desktops—the same breakdown as two years ago.

Social Media News

SEO Guide to Optimizing Your LinkedIn Profile for More Connections, Better Leads

Search Engine Land has put together this handy guide on how to optimize your LinkedIn profile to get more connections and better leads. The author explains that LinkedIn can be a powerful search engine that can drive significant, high-quality traffic to your profile page.

Facebook Unveils Redesign as It Tries to Move Past Privacy Scandals

In the wake of multiple data and privacy scandals affecting millions of its users, Facebook is seeking to increase trust in its platform with a new look geared toward encouraging private and group-based communication rather than use of its News Feed feature. The changes do not amount to a full-scale overhaul, and critics say they are late given how long ago privacy concerns were raised to the company.

Facebook Co-Founder Calls for Breakup of Facebook

Facebook co-founder Chris Hughes wrote a long op-ed in the New York Times, criticizing Facebook for focusing too much on growth and not enough on security and civility. He believes that the company gives too much power to founder and CEO Mark Zuckerberg, and argues that the company should be broken up.

Report: Facebook Looking to Disrupt Credit Cards With Cryptocurrency

Facebook is reportedly trying to raise up to a billion dollars from conventional financial institutions to back a new Facebook-based payment network that would function somewhat like a cryptocurrency. It is not clear exactly how the system would work, but Facebook may seek to position it to compete with credit cards.

Facebook Launches New Tools for Small Businesses

Facebook is targeting small businesses with features focused on automated ad design and management, appointment booking, and video editing for ads. The platform is reportedly home to over 90 million small businesses as of the beginning of 2019.

White House Unveils New Tool to Report Censorship by Social Media Giants

Donald Trump’s administration has released a new form that will allow users to report directly to the White House incidents of supposed censorship by social media platforms such as Facebook, Twitter, Instagram, and YouTube. The move comes as social media companies continue to face pressure from the left for not reacting fast enough to curb hate speech and other harmful content, and criticism from the right for increased moderation of far-right content.

YouTube is Changing how Subscriber Counts are Displayed, Possibly Shifting its Culture

Big changes are coming for high-profile YouTube creators, as the company plans to modify how subscriber counts are shown starting in August. Under the new system, the company will display only approximate rather than exact subscriber numbers, which could signal a change in the platform’s culture. This could also have the effect of rendering obsolete third parties who use YouTube’s API to display subscriber figures.

Legal Industry News

Texas AG Sides With Lawyers Challenging Mandatory Bar Membership

Three Texas lawyers have filed suit on the grounds that mandatory membership in their state bar is a violation of their First Amendment right against coerced speech, and the Texas Attorney General supports their position. The plaintiffs object to having their state bar dues support things like the bar’s diversity programs and access to justice efforts. Similar lawsuits are under way in a number of states, but Texas is the only place where the Attorney General has sided with the plaintiffs.

California Bar Exploring Opportunities to Deploy AI

The State Bar of California has entered into a contract with a private company to explore the use of artificial intelligence (AI) to streamline parts of its attorney discipline system, which has become overloaded with new matters since the Bar began accepting online complaints. The Bar also plans to test the use of AI proctors for bar examinations.

30% of Young Lawyers in This State Wouldn’t Go to Law School in a Do-Over

Almost one-third of Florida lawyers under the age of 36 or who have been practicing less than five years have told the Florida Bar’s Young Lawyers Division that they would not go to law school again given the choice. In response to a survey conducted in late 2018, lawyers cited high levels of student loan debt, long hours, difficult work environments, and mental health issues as affecting their perspective on their experience within the profession.

Has the Supreme Court Eviscerated Recovery of eDiscovery Costs?

In a recent opinion, the U.S. Supreme Court appeared to state that eDiscovery costs may not be recoverable by prevailing parties in litigation. However, the narrow reading the Court gave the text of the relevant statute does not appear to acknowledge or resolve the circuit split that exists on this issue.
